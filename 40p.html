40P. Contact and Non-contact Forces

Key Notes (Synced with Video)

0:00 - 1:03 | What a Force Is
  • A force is a push or pull acting on an object because it interacts with another object.
  • Forces are measured in newtons (N).
  • A force has magnitude, meaning its size or strength.
  • A force also has direction, so forces are vector quantities.
1:04 - 1:27 | Contact and Non-contact Forces
  • All forces can be grouped into contact forces and non-contact forces.
  • Contact forces act only when the objects are physically touching.
  • Non-contact forces can act even when the objects are not touching.
  • This difference is based on whether direct physical contact is needed.
1:27 - 2:34 | Examples of Contact Forces
  • Friction is a contact force because two surfaces must touch for friction to act.
  • Air resistance is a contact force because an object must interact with air particles.
  • Tension is the pulling force transmitted through a rope or string.
  • The normal contact force, or reaction force, supports an object resting on a surface.
  • For example, a table pushes upward on a rock to balance its downward weight.
2:35 - 3:48 | Examples of Non-contact Forces
  • Non-contact forces can act through empty space.
  • The three key examples are gravitational, magnetic, and electrostatic forces.
  • Gravity is an attractive force between objects with mass.
  • Magnetic and electrostatic forces can be attractive or repulsive.
  • These forces can be described as fields of influence around an object.
  • The strength of a non-contact force decreases as the objects move further apart.
