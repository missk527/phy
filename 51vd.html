Notes (Key points from the speech)

Scalar vs vector
  • Scalar: has magnitude only (size/amount). Example: speed 4 m/s; plane flying at 250 m/s.
  • Vector: has magnitude and direction. Example: velocity 6 m/s east; a train traveling backwards at 55 m/s.
  • Speed = scalar, Velocity = vector. Don’t mix them up in exams.
Distance vs displacement
  • Distance (scalar): 10 m, 40 m — magnitude only.
  • Displacement (vector): includes direction, e.g., 40 m east; 2 m downward.
Calculating speed and velocity
  • General idea: divide a change in position measure by the time taken.
  • Speed = distance ÷ time → scalar answer, e.g., 55 m/s.
  • Velocity = displacement ÷ time → vector answer with a direction, e.g., 55 m/s east.
  • Train example: bridge length 550 m crossed in 10 s → speed = 550/10 = 55 m/s. If direction is east, velocity = 55 m/s east.
Symbols used in equations
  • Be careful: the letter S can mean different things in different contexts.
  • In the velocity equation, S is used for displacement.
  • In the speed equation, S stands for speed, while D is distance. In both, T is time.
  • People often use the velocity form even without a stated direction because it allows negative velocity to represent motion backward.
Average values when speed varies
  • Objects often don’t move at constant speed.
  • Average speed = total distance ÷ total time. Average velocity = total displacement ÷ total time.
Typical speeds (m/s)
  • Walking ≈ 1.5 m/s; Running ≈ 3 m/s; Cycling ≈ 6 m/s (varies with age, health, effort).
  • Car (main road) ≈ 25 m/s; Fast train ≈ 55 m/s; Planes > 250 m/s.
  • Sound in air ≈ 330 m/s (memorise); speed changes in other media (e.g., water).
  • Wind (moving air) can vary from ~0 m/s on a still day to faster than a speeding train on windy days; affected by temperature, air pressure, and obstacles (buildings, mountains).
