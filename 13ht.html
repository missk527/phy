13. Hydroelectric Dams & Tidal Barrages

Key Notes (English)

Adapted from the provided speech. Focus: how hydroelectric dams and tidal barrages generate electricity, and their advantages and disadvantages.

1) Basic Idea of Both Systems
  • Both hydroelectric dams and tidal barrages work in basically the same way.
  • They use a dam to stop water flowing normally, creating a higher water level on one side than the other.
  • This difference in height means the stored water has a lot of gravitational potential energy.
  • That energy can be converted into electrical energy using turbines and generators.
2) Hydroelectric Dams
  • In a normal hydroelectric dam, water from the river upstream is trapped by the dam.
  • This stops the water from continuing its journey to the ocean.
  • Over time, a huge volume of water builds up behind the dam, forming a reservoir (like an artificial lake).
  • The water level in the reservoir is much higher than the water level downstream.
3) Tidal Barrages
  • Tides are the regular rise and fall of sea levels, mainly caused by the gravitational pull of the Moon.
  • We usually get two high tides each day, when the sea level rises.
  • Tides affect not only the open ocean but also estuaries, where rivers meet the sea.
  • A tidal barrage is basically a big dam built across an estuary.
  • As the tide comes in, water is trapped behind the barrage.
  • When the tide goes out again, a large difference in water level remains on the two sides of the barrage.
4) Converting Gravitational Energy to Electrical Energy
  • The height difference between the water levels gives the stored water a lot of gravitational potential energy.
  • When water is released from the reservoir (or behind a barrage), it flows downwards through the dam.
  • The force of the moving water spins the blades of turbines inside the dam.
  • These turbines are connected to generators, which produce electrical power as they spin.
  • After passing through the turbines, the water flows out into the river or sea on the other side.
5) Similarities Between Hydroelectric Dams and Tidal Barrages
  • Both use a dam structure to create a difference in water level.
  • Both rely on gravitational potential energy of stored water.
  • Both use turbines and generators to convert energy of moving water into electricity.
  • Both can generate large amounts of electrical power.
6) Main Advantages
  • No pollution while running They generate electricity with no direct air pollution (no greenhouse gases during operation).
  • Reliable Both methods are reliable sources of electricity.
  • Quick response (hydroelectric) Hydroelectric dams in particular can provide an immediate response to an increase in electricity demand.
  • Low running costs Once built, the running costs are fairly low.
  • Flexible scale They can be built on both large and small scales (big power stations or small local schemes).
7) Main Disadvantages – Environmental Impact
  • The main disadvantage is the impact on the environment.
  • Flooding Hydroelectric dams often flood very large areas.
  • This can submerge habitats such as forests, wetlands and farmland.
  • Sometimes entire villages are flooded and people have to move.
  • Blocking movement Both dams and barrages can stop boats and fish from traveling up or down the river.
  • This can damage fish migration routes and river ecosystems.
8) Main Disadvantages – Cost
  • The initial setup cost of building a dam or tidal barrage is usually very high.
  • They require large construction projects, lots of materials and careful design.
  • However, once built, the running costs are relatively low compared with fossil fuel power stations.
9) Summary of Hydroelectric Dams
  • Use a river dam to create a high reservoir of water.
  • Convert gravitational potential energy of stored water into electricity via turbines and generators.
  • Pros: no pollution while running, reliable, very quick response to demand, low running costs.
  • Cons: can flood huge areas, destroy habitats, displace people, and block fish and boats; expensive to build.
10) Summary of Tidal Barrages
  • Use tides (rise and fall of sea level) in estuaries.
  • Trap water at high tide behind a barrage and release it as the tide falls.
  • Like hydroelectric dams, they use turbines and generators.
  • Pros: renewable, no pollution while running, reliable pattern of tides, low running costs.
  • Cons: environmental changes in estuaries, effects on marine life and navigation, high construction cost.
