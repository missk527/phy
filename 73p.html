73P. Sound Waves and Hearing

Key Notes (English)

Rewritten from the speech: sound waves, wave behavior, and human hearing.

1) What Sound Waves Are
  • Sound waves are vibrations that travel through the particles of a medium.
  • They are longitudinal waves, so they move as alternating compressions and rarefactions.
  • Compressions are regions where particles are close together, while rarefactions are regions where particles are spread farther apart.
2) How Sound Travels Through Materials
  • When sound passes through a material, it makes the particles vibrate.
  • These vibrating particles collide with neighboring particles and pass on the vibration.
  • This repeated transfer of vibrations allows the sound wave to move through the medium.
3) Why Density Affects Speed
  • Sound needs particles to travel, so it cannot move through a vacuum.
  • The more closely packed the particles are, the faster sound can be transmitted.
  • That is why sound travels fastest in solids, slower in liquids, and slowest in gases.
4) Sound in a Vacuum
  • A vacuum contains no particles.
  • Because there are no particles to vibrate, sound cannot travel through a vacuum at all.
5) Speed, Frequency, and Wavelength
  • When sound moves from one medium to another, its speed can change.
  • Its frequency stays the same across different media.
  • Using the wave equation, if speed increases and frequency stays constant, the wavelength must increase.
  • So sound has a longer wavelength in denser media and a shorter wavelength in less dense media like air.
6) Reflection, Refraction, and Absorption
  • Sound can be refracted when it changes speed between different media.
  • It can also be reflected or absorbed.
  • Hard, flat surfaces reflect most sound, which is why echoes are produced.
7) How the Ear Receives Sound
  • Sound waves travel along the ear canal and strike the ear drum.
  • The ear drum vibrates and passes the vibrations through the ossicles, the three tiny bones of the middle ear.
  • The vibrations then continue into the cochlea.
  • The cochlea converts the vibrations into electrical signals.
  • These signals travel along the auditory nerve to the brain.
8) Hearing Range and Pitch
  • The brain interprets higher frequencies as higher pitches.
  • More intense signals are interpreted as louder sounds.
  • Human hearing is generally between 20 Hz and 20,000 Hz.
  • Hearing range varies from person to person and usually decreases with age because of wear and tear in the cochlea and auditory nerve.
