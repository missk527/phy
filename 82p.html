82P. Generator Effect

Key Notes (Synced with Video)

0:00 - 0:30 | Generator Effect
  • The generator effect is also called electromagnetic induction.
  • When a wire or coil moves through a magnetic field, a potential difference is induced across it.
  • The induced potential difference can be shown by opposite positive and negative ends of the wire.
  • This happens because the wire experiences a changing magnetic field as it moves.
0:30 - 1:14 | Motion and Direction
  • If the wire stops moving, the induced potential difference disappears.
  • No movement means the magnetic field experienced by the wire is not changing.
  • Changing the direction of movement reverses the direction of the induced potential difference.
  • This is why the positive and negative signs switch when the motion reverses.
1:14 - 1:53 | Complete Circuits
  • A potential difference alone does not make a current unless there is a complete circuit.
  • If the two ends of the wire are joined, electrons can flow around the circuit.
  • The induced potential difference then produces an induced current.
  • Moving the magnets instead of the wire can also induce a current because the magnetic field still changes relative to the wire.
2:01 - 3:11 | Changing the Size of the Induced Voltage
  • No potential difference is induced if the wire does not experience a change in magnetic field.
  • Using stronger magnets creates a stronger magnetic field and a larger induced potential difference.
  • Moving the wire or magnets faster makes the magnetic field change more quickly, increasing the induced potential difference.
  • Adding more turns to the coil also increases the induced potential difference.
3:11 - 3:52 | Summary of Electromagnetic Induction
  • A wire experiences an induced potential difference when it experiences a changing magnetic field.
  • If the wire is part of a closed circuit, the induced potential difference makes a current flow.
  • The induced current can be increased by using a stronger magnetic field.
  • It can also be increased by moving the wire or magnets faster, or by adding more turns to the coil.
3:52 - 4:23 | Magnet Moving Through a Coil
  • Moving a magnet into or out of a coil changes the magnetic field through the coil.
  • This induces a potential difference, and a current flows if the circuit is complete.
  • Reversing the direction of the magnet reverses the direction of the current.
  • Swapping the poles of the magnet also reverses the direction of the induced current.
