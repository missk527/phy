Electric Fields

Key Notes (rewritten from the speech)

1) Electric fields around charges
  • Any charged object creates an electric field in the space around it.
  • This is similar to how masses produce gravitational fields and magnets produce magnetic fields.
  • The field represents a region of influence where the charge can exert a force on other charges.
2) Field lines and direction
  • We draw electric fields using field lines with arrows.
  • Arrows always point from positive to negative.
  • Field lines meet a charged surface at right angles (perpendicular).
  • Draw several lines to show the pattern clearly (e.g., around a point charge).
3) Attraction and repulsion
  • As charges get closer, their fields interact more strongly.
  • Opposite charges attract: you can show this by connecting lines from the positive charge to the negative charge.
  • Like charges repel: field lines spread apart and do not run straight from one like charge to the other.
  • The force between charges is an electrostatic force (electrostatic attraction/repulsion).
4) Ionization of air and sparks
  • Normal air has no overall charge and is usually an electrical insulator.
  • A very strong electric field can strip electrons from nearby air particles, turning them into positive ions.
  • This process is called ionization. Ionized air can conduct electricity.
  • That’s why a spark can travel through the air between strongly charged objects.
