Power — Definitions, Units & Worked Examples

P = E / t
P = W / t
1 W = 1 J s⁻¹

Notes (Exam‑ready key points — rewritten from the speech)

1) Two equivalent definitions of power
  • Power as energy transfer rate: P = \dfrac{E}{t}. It tells you how fast energy is transferred.
  • Power as rate of doing work: P = \dfrac{W}{t}. Same idea, using “work done” instead of energy.
  • When to use which: Use P = E/t for processes with no force–distance description (e.g., electricity to a phone). Use P = W/t when a force moves an object through a distance (e.g., pushing a car).
2) What is “work done”?
  • It’s a measure of energy transferred by a force acting over a distance.
  • Example: pushing a car along the road — the energy you supply is called the work done.
  • Contrast: charging a phone from the mains involves energy transfer but not force × distance, so we simply call it energy transferred.
3) Units & symbols
  • Power (P): watt (W). 1 W = 1 joule per second (1 J s⁻¹).
  • Energy (E) / Work done (W): joule (J). 1 kJ = 1000 J.
  • Time (t): second (s). Convert minutes to seconds by ×60.
4) Lamp comparison (which is more powerful?)
  • Lamp A transfers 1200 J in 20 s ⇒ P = 1200/20 = 60 W.
  • Lamp B transfers 1500 J in 30 s ⇒ P = 1500/30 = 50 W.
  • Therefore, Lamp A (60 W) is more powerful than Lamp B (50 W).
5) Rearranging & using the formula
  • From P = E/t you can get E = P × t and t = E/P.
  • Microwave example: 1100 W for 3 min = 180 s ⇒ E = 1100 × 180 = 198000 J = 198 kJ.
  • Car‑push example: 9 kJ of work in 20 s ⇒ P = 9000/20 = 450 W.
6) Quick exam tips
  • Always check units: convert min → s; kJ ↔ J.
  • Pick the matching definition: use “work done” language when a force moves an object.
  • Quote power in W (or kW); energy/work in J (or kJ); time in s.
