Stopping Distance: Thinking Distance & Braking Distance

Key Notes (rewritten from the speech)

1) What is stopping distance?
  • Stopping distance is the minimum distance a vehicle needs to stop in an emergency.
  • Total stopping distance has two parts:
    Total stopping distance = Thinking distance + Braking distance
  • In exams, you must clearly distinguish between:
    • Thinking distance – before the brakes are applied.
    • Braking distance – after the brakes are applied.
2) Thinking distance & reaction time
  • Thinking distance is how far the vehicle travels during the driver’s reaction time.
  • Reaction time is the time between:
    • the driver seeing a hazard and
    • the driver actually pressing the brake pedal.
Thinking distance depends on: • Speed of the vehicle • Driver’s reaction time If speed ↑ or reaction time ↑ → thinking distance ↑
  • Reaction time varies from person to person.
  • Anything that reduces alertness tends to increase reaction time, for example:
    Tiredness Alcohol (drunk) Drugs Distractions (e.g. using a phone)
  • If reaction time increases, thinking distance increases.
3) Braking distance & key factors
  • Braking distance is the distance the car travels after the brakes are applied until it comes to a stop.
  • It is the distance covered under the braking force (under normal conditions).
Main factors affecting braking distance: 1. Speed of the vehicle 2. Mass of the vehicle 3. Condition of the brakes 4. Condition of the tyres and road surface
  • Speed and mass:
    • Both affect the vehicle’s kinetic energy.
    • The more kinetic energy the vehicle has, the more energy must be removed to stop it.
    • Higher speed or greater mass → longer braking distance.
  • Condition of the brakes:
    • Worn or faulty brakes cannot apply as much force.
    • The car slows down more slowly and travels further before stopping.
  • Traction between tyres and road:
    • If the road is wet or icy, there is less friction.
    • Low friction can cause the car to skid.
    • Bald tyres (no tread) cannot grip the road well.
    • Poor tyre condition also increases braking distance.
4) Effect of speed on stopping distance
  • We can show stopping distance using diagrams:
    • Each car has a purple section = thinking distance.
    • Each car has a red section = braking distance.
  • Thinking distance:
    • Increases proportionally with speed.
    • If speed doubles, thinking distance also roughly doubles.
  • Braking distance:
    • Increases more quickly than thinking distance.
    • If you double the speed, braking distance becomes about four times larger.
    • If you triple the speed, braking distance becomes about nine times larger.
Braking distance ∝ speed² (it increases with the square of speed) Double speed → braking distance × 4 Triple speed → braking distance × 9
  • As a result, a graph of total stopping distance vs speed is curved, not a straight line.
  • The graph gets steeper as speed increases.
  • Key message: If you are travelling fast, it will take much longer to stop than you might expect.
