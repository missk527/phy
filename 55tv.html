Terminal Velocity: Forces, Drag & Parachutes

Key Notes (rewritten from the speech)

What is terminal velocity?
  • Terminal velocity is when a falling object’s velocity becomes constant—it no longer accelerates or decelerates.
  • At terminal velocity, the resultant force is zero: weight downward equals air resistance (drag) upward.
Step 1 — Just after the jump
  • At the instant a person steps out of a plane, the only force is weight (gravity), so the resultant force is downward.
  • They accelerate downward because weight is unopposed.
Step 2 — Building air resistance (drag)
  • As the person starts to fall, they collide with air particles → an upward force called air resistance / drag.
  • Drag depends on surface area (bigger area → more collisions) and velocity (faster → more collisions).
  • As velocity increases, drag increases; weight stays constant → resultant force decreases, so acceleration gets smaller.
Step 3 — Reaching terminal velocity
  • Eventually, drag = weight → resultant force = 0 → acceleration = 0.
  • Velocity becomes constant: this is terminal velocity.
  • On a velocity–time graph: the curve’s slope decreases and then flattens when terminal velocity is reached.
Parachute opening & new terminal velocity
  • Opening a parachute greatly increases surface area → drag jumps up.
  • For a moment, drag > weight → resultant force points upward → the person decelerates (slows) while still moving downward.
  • As they slow, drag decreases until it equals weight again → a new, lower terminal velocity is reached.
Recap
  • Start: weight ≫ drag → accelerate downward.
  • Mid-fall: speed up → drag ↑ → acceleration ↓.
  • Terminal: drag = weight → a = 0 → constant velocity.
  • Parachute: surface area ↑ → drag ↑↑ → slow down → lower terminal velocity.
