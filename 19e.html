Circuit Symbols & Components

Key Notes (rewritten from the speech)

1) Power sources and controlling current
  • To supply electrical energy to a circuit, you use a cell or a battery (multiple cells).
  • A switch controls whether current can flow:
    • Closed switch = the circuit is complete, so current flows.
    • Open switch = the circuit is broken, so current stops.
Key idea • Current only flows when the circuit is complete (closed loop). • An open switch effectively turns the circuit off.
2) Common components: lamps, fuses, diodes and LEDs
  • A filament lamp (bulb) converts electrical energy into light and heat.
  • A fuse is a safety device: if too much current flows, it melts/breaks and stops the circuit.
  • A diode only allows current to flow in one direction.
  • An LED (light-emitting diode) is a diode that gives off light when current flows in the forward direction.
  • LEDs are used in everyday tech like alarm clocks, traffic lights, and LED bulbs.

3) Measuring in circuits: ammeters and voltmeters
  • An ammeter measures current and must be connected in series.
  • A voltmeter measures potential difference (voltage) and must be connected in parallel across a component.
Connections • Ammeter → series (so the same current flows through it). • Voltmeter → parallel (so it compares potential difference across a component).
4) Resistors: fixed and variable
  • A resistor limits current by providing resistance (measured in ohms, Ω).
  • Fixed resistors have a set resistance value.
  • Variable resistors can be adjusted to change the resistance (and therefore the current) easily.
Ohm (Ω) • Unit of resistance. • Higher resistance → smaller current (for the same voltage).
5) Sensor resistors: LDRs and thermistors
  • An LDR (light-dependent resistor) changes resistance based on light intensity:
    • Bright light → low resistance → more current can flow.
    • Darkness → high resistance → very little current flows.
  • LDRs are useful in automatic night lights and burglar alarms (light detected → resistance drops → current increases).
  • A thermistor changes resistance based on temperature:
    • Higher temperature → lower resistance.
    • Lower temperature → higher resistance.
  • Thermistors act as temperature sensors in car engines and electronic thermostats.
Sensor summary • LDR: light ↑ → resistance ↓ • Thermistor: temperature ↑ → resistance ↓
