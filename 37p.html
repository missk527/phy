37P. Irradiation & Contamination

Key Notes (Synced with Video)

0:00 - 0:45 | Irradiation
  • Irradiation happens when an object is exposed to radiation.
  • Radiation can be ionizing or non-ionizing.
  • Examples include alpha, beta, gamma rays, X-rays, ultraviolet, and microwaves.
  • If radiation reaches your body, you have been irradiated.
0:45 - 1:21 | Contamination
  • Contamination happens when radioactive particles get onto objects.
  • Example: radioactive uranium on your skin.
  • The main danger is that radioactive materials decay and irradiate you.
  • Contamination increases the risk of radiation exposure.
1:21 - 2:17 | Harmful Effects of Radiation
  • The danger depends on radiation type, exposure location, and dosage.
  • Ionizing radiation is more dangerous because it can enter living cells.
  • Radiation can ionize DNA and cause mutations.
  • Mutations may lead to uncontrolled cell division and cancer.
  • Ultraviolet radiation can also increase cancer risk.
2:17 - 3:19 | Radiation Types & Penetration
  • Alpha radiation is most harmful inside the body.
  • Alpha particles are stopped by air and skin.
  • Beta and gamma radiation can penetrate skin.
  • External beta and gamma sources are dangerous.
  • Internal contamination makes alpha radiation very dangerous.
3:19 - 3:57 | Radiation Dosage
  • Dosage depends on distance, exposure time, and radioactivity.
  • Greater distance reduces exposure.
  • Longer exposure increases risk.
  • Highly radioactive materials are more dangerous.
3:57 - 4:25 | Radiation Safety
  • Protective clothing helps reduce contamination.
  • Gloves and overalls are commonly used.
  • Tongs should be used instead of bare hands.
  • Radioactive materials are often stored in lead-lined containers.
4:25 - 4:42 | Important Final Point
  • Being irradiated does not make someone radioactive.
  • People exposed to radiation cannot emit radiation afterwards.
  • Only contamination with radioactive material can harm others.
