Notes (Key points from the speech)

Radioactive decay
  • Some materials contain unstable isotopes that become more stable by emitting radiation.
  • Radioactive decay can produce alpha particles, beta particles, or gamma rays.
  • The decay process of a single radioactive isotope is completely random.
Activity
  • Activity means the overall rate of decay in a radioactive sample.
  • Activity is measured in becquerels (Bq).
  • One becquerel means one decay per second.
  • A sample with an activity of 600 Bq has 600 decays every second.
Half-life
  • Half-life is the time taken for the number of radioactive nuclei to halve.
  • It can also mean the time taken for the activity to halve.
  • Fewer radioactive nuclei means a lower activity.
  • As radioactive particles decay, the sample becomes less active over time.
Decay graphs
  • Graphs of activity against time show a curved decrease, not a straight line.
  • The activity decreases more slowly over time because fewer unstable nuclei remain.
  • The half-life can be found by measuring the time for the activity to halve.
  • Example: activity dropping from 600 Bq to 300 Bq in 2 hours means the half-life is 2 hours.
Comparing radioactive samples
  • A sample with a higher starting activity decays more quickly.
  • A faster decrease in activity means a shorter half-life.
  • Different radioactive isotopes have different half-lives.
Measuring activity
  • A Geiger–Müller tube and counter can detect radiation.
  • It records alpha particles, beta particles, and gamma rays.
  • The reading is called the count rate.
  • The count rate is used to estimate the activity of a sample.
Half-life calculations
  • To solve half-life questions, first find the number of half-lives that have passed.
  • Then halve the number of radioactive nuclei for each half-life.
  • Example: 3 million nuclei with a half-life of 40 hours becomes 375,000 nuclei after 120 hours.
