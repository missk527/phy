Electric Circuits: Current, Voltage & Resistance

Key Notes (rewritten from the speech)

1) What is an electric circuit?
  • An electric circuit is a closed loop that allows charge (electrons) to flow.
  • It must include a power source (e.g. cell or battery) and a conducting path (wires).
  • The simplest circuit is: cell + wires + component (e.g. a lamp) all connected in a loop.
  • We usually represent circuits using circuit diagrams with standard symbols.
2) Basic circuit symbols
  • A cell is a single power source. Its symbol has a long line (positive) and a short line (negative).
  • A battery is two or more cells joined together. Its symbol is just several cell symbols in a row.
  • In exams, cells and batteries behave in the same way – they both provide potential difference.
Cell Battery (two or more cells) Filament lamp (light bulb) Switch (open / closed)
  • A filament lamp is a small light bulb; if the circuit is complete and powered, it lights up.
  • A switch can be:
    • Closed – circuit complete, current can flow, lamp is on.
    • Open – circuit broken, current stops, lamp is off.
3) Electric current (I)
  • Current is the rate of flow of charge (the flow of electrons) around a circuit.
  • It is denoted by the letter I.
  • It is measured in amperes (amps), symbol A.
  • You can think of current like the flow of water through a pipe.
Current (I) • Measures how much charge passes a point each second. • Unit: ampere (A). • Analogy: water flow in a pipe.
4) Potential difference / voltage (V)
  • Potential difference (p.d.) is the driving force that pushes electrons around the circuit.
  • It is provided by the cell or battery.
  • It is denoted by the letter V.
  • Another common name for potential difference is voltage.
  • It is measured in volts, symbol V.
  • Analogy: it is like the pump that pushes water along a pipe.
Potential difference (V) • "Push" provided by the power source. • Unit: volt (V). • Also called voltage.
5) Resistance (R)
  • Resistance is anything that opposes or resists the flow of electrons.
  • It is denoted by the letter R.
  • It is measured in ohms, symbol Ω.
  • Analogy: resistance is like a partial blockage in a water pipe, making it harder for water to flow.
Resistance (R) • Opposes current in a circuit. • Unit: ohm (Ω). • Analogy: a blockage in a pipe.
6) Direction of electron flow vs conventional current
  • In a cell symbol, the longer line is the positive terminal.
  • The shorter line is the negative terminal.
  • Electrons are negatively charged, so they actually flow from the negative terminal to the positive terminal.
  • Historically, people did not know about electrons, so they said current flows from positive to negative.
  • This is called conventional current, and we still use this definition in circuits and exams.
Electron flow: negative → positive Conventional current: positive → negative (For exams, always use conventional current direction.)
