Seismic Waves

Key Notes (rewritten from the speech)

1) Seismic waves
  • Large-scale events such as earthquakes, volcanoes, and explosions produce waves that travel through the Earth.
  • These waves spread out in all directions and move through different layers of the planet.
  • They are called seismic waves.
2) P waves and S waves
  • P waves are longitudinal waves and can travel through both solids and liquids.
  • S waves are transverse waves and can travel only through solids.
  • P waves also travel faster than S waves.
3) How scientists study the Earth
  • Scientists study how seismic waves move through the Earth to learn about its internal structure.
  • This helps them understand what the Earth is made of.
  • Special devices called seismometers are used to detect the waves.
4) Comparing wave data
  • Seismologists compare results from different places around the world.
  • By measuring how long waves take to travel from an earthquake to different locations, they get clues about the Earth’s layers.
  • The pattern of which waves arrive and where they arrive is very important.
5) Reflection, absorption, transmission, and refraction
  • When seismic waves meet a boundary between different materials, they can be reflected, absorbed, or transmitted.
  • If they are transmitted, they are often refracted, which means they change direction.
  • Refraction happens because waves change speed in materials with different densities.
6) Wave paths inside the Earth
  • The Earth’s layers do not all have the same density, so seismic waves are constantly being bent.
  • Within one layer, waves are usually refracted gradually and seem to curve.
  • At the boundary between two layers, such as the mantle and outer core, the change in direction is more sudden.
7) Evidence for a liquid outer core
  • P waves can pass through the liquid outer core, but S waves cannot.
  • This was one of the key clues showing that the outer core is liquid.
  • S waves cannot reach the inner core because the liquid outer core blocks them.
8) Wave detection and Earth’s structure
  • S waves are missing from certain regions because they cannot travel through the liquid outer core.
  • P waves are detected in most places, but there is also a region where they are not detected because refraction bends them away.
  • By studying where P waves and S waves are or are not detected, scientists worked out the Earth’s internal structure.
