15. V = IR & I–V Graphs

Key Notes (English)

Adapted from the provided speech. Focus: the equation V = IR, how to use it, and how to interpret current–potential difference (I–V) graphs for resistors, filament lamps and diodes.

1) The Equation V = IR (Ohm’s Law Form)
  • The equation V = IR links three key electrical quantities.
  • V = potential difference (voltage), measured in volts (V).
  • I = current, measured in amperes (A).
  • R = resistance, measured in ohms (Ω).
  • In words, it means: voltage = current × resistance.
2) Using V = IR – Basic Calculations
  • If we know any two of V, I and R, we can calculate the third.
  • Example 1: A circuit has a current of 4 A and a resistance of 2 Ω.
  • Using V = IR, the potential difference is V = 4 × 2 = 8 V.
  • Example 2: A 24 V battery produces a current of 8 A.
  • To find resistance, rearrange to R = V / I and calculate R = 24 / 8 = 3 Ω.
3) Rearranging the Equation
  • From V = IR we can rearrange to find different quantities:
  • To find current: I = V / R.
  • To find resistance: R = V / I.
  • These forms are essential when solving circuit problems.
4) Proportional Relationship Between V and I
  • As long as the resistance stays constant, voltage and current are directly proportional.
  • If potential difference increases, the current increases by the same factor, and vice versa.
  • This relationship can be shown on a graph of current (I) against potential difference (V).
5) I–V Graphs for Ohmic Conductors (Wires and Resistors)
  • An ohmic conductor is a component whose resistance stays constant as long as conditions (like temperature) stay the same.
  • Examples include simple metal wires and fixed resistors at constant temperature.
  • Their I–V graphs are straight lines through the origin.
  • The straight line shows that current is proportional to potential difference.
  • The negative part of the line (below the axis) represents the same behaviour when the battery is connected the other way round, so V and I are negative.
6) Gradient and Resistance
  • On an I–V graph, the steepness (gradient) tells us about the resistance.
  • A steeper line means a larger current for the same voltage and therefore a smaller resistance.
  • A less steep line means smaller current for the same voltage and therefore a larger resistance.
  • For example, a 10 Ω resistor would have a line that is less steep than a smaller resistor.
7) Effect of Temperature on Resistance
  • When we draw straight I–V graphs for wires and resistors, we assume the temperature is constant.
  • If the temperature increases, the resistance also increases.
  • In real circuits, high currents can cause wires to heat up over time.
  • Because of this heating, the I–V line may not stay perfectly straight in practice.
8) Filament Lamp I–V Graph
  • A filament lamp is a light bulb containing a very thin metal filament.
  • As current flows through the filament, it heats up until it is hot enough to emit light.
  • The high temperature causes the resistance to increase a lot.
  • On an I–V graph, the curve is steep at low voltages but becomes less steep as voltage and current increase.
  • This shows that less current flows per volt at higher voltages because the filament is hotter and more resistive.
9) Diode I–V Graph
  • A diode is a component that allows current to flow in only one direction.
  • When the potential difference is in the forward direction (positive), the diode allows current to flow once a certain voltage is reached.
  • In the reverse direction, the diode has a very high resistance.
  • Because of this, almost no current flows when the potential difference is negative.
  • On the I–V graph, we see current only on the positive side of the voltage axis.
10) Summary of Key Ideas
  • V = IR links voltage, current and resistance and allows us to calculate any one if we know the other two.
  • For a constant resistance, the I–V graph is a straight line showing direct proportionality.
  • Higher resistance means a less steep I–V line.
  • Filament lamps heat up, so their resistance increases and their I–V graph is a curved line that flattens.
  • Diodes conduct mainly in one direction, giving an I–V graph with current on the positive side only.
