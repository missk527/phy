Notes (Key points rewritten from the speech)

1) What distance–time graphs show
  • They show how far an object travels over time (e.g., a cyclist’s journey).
  • From the entire graph you can read total distance and total time.
  • Different segments reveal different stages of motion.
2) Gradient = speed (core idea)
  • Gradient = change in distance ÷ change in time.
  • That is exactly the formula for speed → therefore gradient ≡ speed.
  • Example: 20 m in 2 s → gradient = 20/2 = 10 m s⁻¹ (straight line ⇒ constant speed).
3) Interpreting common shapes
  • Straight sloping line → constant speed.
  • Flat line (horizontal) → stationary (speed = 0).
  • Increasing steepness → accelerating (speed increasing).
  • Decreasing steepness → decelerating (speed decreasing).
4) Instantaneous speed on a curve (tangent)
  • To find speed at a particular time on a curve, draw a tangent at that point.
  • Compute its gradient using two points far apart on the tangent: Δdistance / Δtime.
  • Example: Δdistance ≈ 12 m over Δtime = 3 s → speed ≈ 4 m s⁻¹.
5) Exam tips
  • Use clear scales and units (m, s).
  • Pick tangent points as far apart as possible to reduce percentage error.
  • On straight segments, just use the segment’s rise/run for speed.
