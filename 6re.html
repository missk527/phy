Reducing Unwanted Energy Transfers

Thermal insulation
Lubrication
Streamlining

Notes (Exam-ready key points, rewritten from the speech)

1) Goal: reduce unwanted energy transfers
  • We aim to keep useful energy where we want it (e.g., warmth inside a house) and prevent losses to the surroundings.
  • Focus: thermal insulation (cut heat losses by conduction & convection) and lubrication/streamlining (cut losses from friction & air resistance).
2) Stop convection heat loss: make the building airtight
  • Convection transfers heat in fluids (air & liquids). Warm air escaping is a major route of heat loss.
  • Door/window foam seals reduce drafts, so less warm air leaves and less cold air enters.
  • Curtains at night help reduce air movement near windows (less convective loss).
3) Cut conduction through solids: use thick, low-conductivity materials
  • Conduction transfers heat through solids. Thick walls of low thermal conductivity reduce the rate of energy transfer.
  • Cavity walls: two brick layers with a small air gap reduce conduction, because air is a poor conductor.
  • But plain air can allow convection inside the gap; the fix is to fill the cavity with insulating foam containing many tiny, isolated air bubbles → little conduction and little convection.
4) Windows: single vs double glazing
  • Single glazing: one pane of glass → heat easily lost by conduction.
  • Double glazing: two panes separated by a tiny gap (air or inert gas) → the gap blocks conduction and suppresses convection → lower heat loss.
5) Reduce friction losses
  • Friction is the resistive force when surfaces slide or an object moves through a fluid; it wastes energy (often as heating) and reduces efficiency.
  • Lubrication (e.g., oil on a bike chain & cogs) forms a thin layer between moving parts, reducing friction so less energy is dissipated and motion is easier.
  • Streamlining vehicles reduces air resistance (drag), so less fuel is needed for the same speed → better efficiency (applies to cars, planes, etc.).
6) Exam tips
  • Match the mechanism of loss to the solution: convection → seals/curtains; conduction → insulating materials/cavity foam; friction/air resistance → lubrication/streamlining.
  • Use keywords: “low thermal conductivity”, “air is a poor conductor”, “air gap”, “insulating foam with isolated bubbles”, “reduce drag”.
  • Explain why each method works (e.g., “reduces convection by limiting air movement,” “reduces conduction by adding low‑k layers”).
