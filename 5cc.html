Heat Transfer — Conduction, Convection & Radiation

Key Notes (rewritten from the speech)

1) Heating & Temperature
  • When an object is heated, energy is transferred to its thermal energy store and its temperature increases.
  • Three transfer pathways depending on the medium: conduction (solids), convection (fluids: liquids & gases), radiation (through empty space).

2) Conduction (mainly in solids)
  • Idea: neighboring particles transfer energy via collisions as their vibrations increase.
  • Heating one end of a metal → particles there gain kinetic energy → vibrate more → collide with neighbors → energy passes along the solid.
  • Conduction is strongest in solids because particles are closely packed, so collisions are frequent.
  • Thermal conductivity: a measure of how well a material conducts heat. Metals = high; plastics = low (insulators); fluids generally = low.
3) Convection (in fluids: liquids & gases)
  • Particles are free to move. When heated they gain kinetic energy, move faster, and spread by random motion (diffusion).
  • Warmer fluid becomes less dense (expands) and rises; cooler, denser fluid sinks to replace it.
  • This continuous cycle forms a convection current while heating continues.
  • Examples: radiators warming room air; large-scale currents in oceans and within buildings.
  • To reduce convection, restrict fluid flow (e.g., blankets trap warm air, stopping it from escaping).
4) Radiation (infrared)
  • Heat transfer can occur without particles → travels through a vacuum as infrared radiation.
  • All objects both emit and absorb infrared radiation at the same time.
  • Hotter objects emit more radiation. (e.g., feeling heat from a barbecue without touching it.)
  • Infrared is part of the electromagnetic spectrum (covered elsewhere).
5) Conduction vs Convection
  • Both involve particles gaining kinetic energy.
  • In conduction, energy transfers between particles; the particles themselves stay in place (mainly in solids).
  • In convection, particles move and carry energy with them (in fluids).
