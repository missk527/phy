Efficiency — Energy & Power Useful vs Wasted · % and Decimal · Conservation of Energy

Video + exam-focused notes + 40-question practice

Notes (Key points from the speech)

What is efficiency?
  • Efficiency is the proportion of the input energy (or power) that is transferred into useful output.
  • Devices are rarely 100% efficient → some energy becomes wasted (often heat).
  • Lamp example: Input: electrical → Outputs: light (useful) + heat (wasted).
Formulas you must know
  • Energy version: Efficiency = Useful energy output / Total energy input
  • Power version: Efficiency = Useful power output / Total power input
  • Express as a decimal (0 to 1) or a percentage (×100%).
  • Values > 1 or > 100% are impossible (would violate conservation of energy).
Worked examples from the speech
  • Lamps (each 300 J input):
    • Incandescent: 45 J light → efficiency = 45/300 = 0.15 (15%)
    • LED: 225 J light → efficiency = 225/300 = 0.75 (75%)
    • The LED is 0.75 / 0.15 = 5× as efficient.
  • Microwave (power): η = 70% = 0.70; Pin = 800 W → Puseful = 0.70 × 800 = 560 W.
Common pitfalls
  • Dividing the wrong way round gives η > 1 (>100%) → impossible.
  • For percent, remember to ×100 (or ÷100 to get decimal).
Special note on heaters
  • No real device is perfectly efficient because some energy becomes waste heat.
  • Exception in context: If the device’s intended output is heat (e.g., an electric heater), then that thermal energy is useful, not waste.
