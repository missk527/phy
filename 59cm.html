59. Momentum & Conservation of Momentum

Key Notes (English)

Adapted from the provided speech. Focus: definition of momentum, vector nature, conservation of momentum, collision examples, and recoil problems like the gun–bullet example.

1) What Is Momentum?
  • Momentum is a property that all moving objects have.
  • It depends on both the mass of the object and its velocity.
  • The basic equation is momentum = mass × velocity.
  • In symbols: p = m × v, where p is momentum, m is mass, and v is velocity.
  • The SI unit for momentum is kg m/s.
2) Example: Dinosaur and Car
  • Example 1: A 4500 kg dinosaur12 m/s.
  • Its momentum is p = 4500 × 12 = 54,000 kg m/s.
  • Example 2: A 1200 kg car moving at 25 m/s.
  • Its momentum is p = 1200 × 25 = 30,000 kg m/s.
3) Momentum Is a Vector Quantity
  • Momentum is a vector, which means it has both magnitude and direction.
  • We usually choose one direction (for example, to the right) as positive.
  • Objects moving in the opposite direction then have negative momentum.
  • In the example, if we take right as positive, the dinosaur moving right has positive momentum.
  • The car moving left in the same diagram would have negative momentum.
4) Conservation of Momentum – The Principle
  • The principle of conservation of momentum says that in a closed system, the total momentum before an event is equal to the total momentum after the event.
  • This applies to events like collisions and explosions.
  • Mathematically: total momentum before = total momentum after.
5) Head-On Collision Example – Dinosaur and Car
  • Imagine the dinosaur and the car moving towards each other and then colliding.
  • We treat them as two particles that stick together and move with a shared velocity after the collision.
  • Step 1: Find total momentum before collision by adding their momenta with signs.
  • Using right as positive: dinosaur has +54,000 kg m/s; car has −30,000 kg m/s.
  • Total momentum before = +54,000 + (−30,000) = +24,000 kg m/s.
  • By conservation of momentum, total momentum after the collision is also +24,000 kg m/s.
6) Finding the Shared Velocity After Collision
  • After they collide and stick together, we treat dinosaur + car as one combined object.
  • Combined mass = 4500 kg + 1200 kg = 5700 kg.
  • We rearrange p = m v to v = p / m.
  • So v = 24,000 ÷ 5700 ≈ 4.2–4.4 m/s to the right (depending on rounding).
  • This means they both move off together to the right after the collision.
7) Situations with Zero Initial Momentum – Recoil
  • If the total momentum before an event is zero, the total momentum after must also be zero.
  • Stationary objects have zero momentum because their velocity is zero.
  • Example: A gun before it is fired has zero momentum.
  • When it fires, the bullet gains forward momentum, so the gun must gain backward momentum to keep the total at zero.
  • This backwards motion of the gun is called recoil.
8) Gun and Bullet Example – Working Out Recoil
  • Suppose a gun of mass 2.0 kg fires a 5 g bullet (0.005 kg) forwards.
  • The bullet's velocity is 120 m/s forwards.
  • Bullet momentum = 0.005 × 120 = 0.6 kg m/s forwards.
  • Let the gun's velocity be v (backwards). Its momentum is then 2v.
  • Total momentum after must be zero: 2v + 0.6 = 0.
  • Rearrange: 2v = −0.6, so v = −0.3 m/s.
  • The negative sign shows the gun recoils in the opposite direction to the bullet.
9) Symbol for Momentum
  • The letter symbol used for momentum is p (often shown as the Greek letter rho, but it looks like p).
  • The momentum equation can be written as p = m v.
  • Always include a direction or sign when working with momentum in 1D problems.
10) Problem-Solving Tips
  • Choose one direction as positive at the start and stick to it.
  • Assign positive and negative velocities according to that chosen direction.
  • Use p = m v to find individual momenta, then add them to get the total.
  • Apply conservation of momentum to link momentum before and after the event.
  • Rearrange the equation to solve for the unknown velocity.
