57. Newton's Third Law

Key Notes (English)

Adapted from the provided speech. Focus: the statement of Newton's third law, what “equal and opposite” means, and how it links to acceleration via F = ma.

1) Newton’s Third Law – Definition
  • Newton’s third law states that when two objects interact, the forces they exert on each other are equal and opposite.
  • This means:
    • They are equal in magnitude (size).
    • They are opposite in direction.
  • This is the definition you can use directly in an exam.
2) Equal and Opposite – What It Means
  • The “equal” part refers to the size (magnitude) of the forces.
  • The “opposite” part refers to their directions.
  • Example:
    • If you push a box with a force of 100 N,
    • the box pushes back on you with a force of 100 N in the opposite direction.
3) Normal Contact Force
  • The force that the box exerts on you is called the normal contact force.
  • It acts at the point of contact between two solid objects.
  • Normal contact forces are one example of a Newton’s third law pair.
4) Which Object Moves?
  • Even though the forces are equal and opposite, the motion of the objects can be very different.
  • If the box is small (low mass), the box is more likely to be the one that moves.
  • If the box is very big (large mass), it might hardly move at all, and you might be pushed backwards instead.
  • Sometimes both objects move a bit (for example with a medium-sized box).
5) Everyday Examples
  • When you push yourself off a wall:
    • You push the wall, and the wall pushes you back with an equal and opposite force.
    • You are the one that moves away from the wall.
  • When you jump off the ground:
    • Your feet push down on the ground.
    • The ground pushes you upwards with an equal and opposite force, so you move up.
  • In each case, the forces are equal and opposite, but the effect (the motion) depends on mass.
6) Link to Newton’s Second Law
  • Newton’s second law is F = ma, where:
    • F is the resultant force (N)
    • m is the mass (kg)
    • a is the acceleration (m/s²)
  • Rearranging gives a = F / m.
  • This shows that acceleration depends on the force and the mass.
7) Why Smaller Objects Move More
  • For two objects pushing on each other, the forces are equal (same F).
  • However, their masses can be different.
  • From a = F / m:
    • If m is small, then a is larger.
    • If m is large, then a is smaller.
  • So the object with the smaller mass will generally have the larger acceleration and move more.
  • This explains why a light box moves more easily than a heavy box when you push.
8) Not “One Force Only” – Always Pairs
  • Newton’s third law always involves a pair of forces on two different objects.
  • It is wrong to think “you push the box, but the box does nothing”.
  • The box always pushes back on you with an equal and opposite force.
9) Motion vs Force – Key Idea
  • Equal and opposite forces do not automatically mean equal motion.
  • Motion depends on both the force and the mass (via F = ma).
  • Therefore:
    • A light object can accelerate a lot.
    • A heavy object may accelerate very little, even with the same force.
10) Key Takeaways
  • Newton’s third law: when two objects interact, they exert equal and opposite forces on each other.
  • Equal = same magnitude; opposite = opposite direction.
  • Examples include pushing a box, pushing off a wall, or jumping off the ground.
  • Which object moves more depends on mass, using F = ma and a = F / m.
  • The smaller mass generally gets the larger acceleration and moves most.
