83P. Transformers

Key Notes (Synced with Video)

0:00 - 0:52 | Transformers in the National Grid
  • Most electricity in the UK is generated at large power stations across the country.
  • Before electricity travels through the National Grid, step-up transformers increase the voltage to about 400,000 V.
  • Using a very high voltage helps reduce energy losses as electricity travels through long cables and pylons.
  • Near homes and towns, step-down transformers reduce the voltage to about 230 V so it is safer to use.
0:52 - 1:20 | The Main Job and Parts of a Transformer
  • A transformer changes the potential difference, or voltage, of an alternating current supply.
  • A transformer can either increase voltage or decrease voltage.
  • The main parts are a primary coil, a secondary coil, and an iron core.
  • The iron core links the magnetic effects of the two coils.
1:20 - 2:43 | How a Transformer Works
  • An alternating potential difference is applied across the primary coil, causing an alternating current to flow.
  • This alternating current produces an alternating magnetic field around the primary coil.
  • The alternating magnetic field magnetises the iron core and induces an alternating magnetic field in it.
  • The changing magnetic field in the iron core induces a potential difference across the secondary coil.
  • If the secondary coil is part of a complete circuit, an alternating current flows in the secondary circuit.
2:43 - 3:16 | Energy Transfer by Magnetic Fields
  • A transformer transfers electrical energy from the primary coil to the secondary coil using induced magnetic fields.
  • Electricity does not conduct directly from one coil to the other.
  • The wires are insulated, so charge cannot pass between the coils and the iron core.
  • The magnetic field provides the link between the two separate circuits.
3:16 - 3:46 | Step-up and Step-down Transformers
  • Transformers can change voltage because the primary and secondary coils can have different numbers of turns.
  • In a step-up transformer, the secondary coil has more turns than the primary coil, so the output voltage is higher.
  • For example, if the secondary coil has twice as many turns, a 10 V input can become a 20 V output.
  • In a step-down transformer, the secondary coil has fewer turns than the primary coil, so the output voltage is lower.
