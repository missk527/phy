Series Circuits: Current, Voltage & Resistance

Key Notes (rewritten from the speech)

1) Series vs parallel circuits
  • A key exam confusion is the difference between series and parallel circuits.
  • In a series circuit, there is only one loop and all components are connected one after another in that loop.
  • In a parallel circuit, there is more than one loop or branch.
Series circuit • Single loop • Components in a line, one after another Parallel circuit • More than one loop (branches) • Components on different branches
  • This small structural difference changes how we measure and think about:
    Current Voltage (potential difference) Resistance
  • This video focuses on series circuits. Parallel circuits are considered in the next video.
2) Features and disadvantages of series circuits
  • In a series circuit, all components share the same loop.
  • If any one component is disconnected or broken, the entire circuit stops working.
Key disadvantage of series circuits • One component fails → current stops everywhere → whole circuit off
  • Because of this major disadvantage, very few practical devices are actually connected in series.
3) Voltage (potential difference) in series circuits
  • The potential difference (voltage) of the cell or battery is shared across all components in series.
Total voltage in series Vtotal = V1 + V2 + V3 + ... • The sum of the voltages across each component equals the battery voltage.
  • Example: if the battery has 12 V and there are only two lamps in series:
Example 1 • Total battery voltage = 12 V • Lamp A has 8 V across it • Lamp B must have 4 V Because 8 V + 4 V = 12 V
Example 2 (identical lamps) • Total battery voltage = 12 V • Two identical lamps with same resistance • Each lamp gets 6 V Because they share the voltage equally
4) Current in series circuits
  • In a series circuit, the current is the same everywhere in the loop.
  • No matter where you measure it in the series circuit, the value of current is identical.
  • Current is measured using an ammeter.
  • An ammeter is placed in series, that is, in the main loop with the other components.
Current in series • Same at every point in the loop • Same through every component • Measured with an ammeter in series
  • Because the current through all components is the same, it does not matter where in the loop you place the ammeter.
  • We can also calculate current using Ohm's law by dividing the total voltage by the total resistance.
Ohm's law (rearranged) I = Vtotal / Rtotal
5) Resistance in series and example calculation
  • In a series circuit, the total resistance is the sum of the individual resistances.
Total resistance in series Rtotal = R1 + R2 + R3 + ... (ΣR means "sum of all resistances")
  • Example: a 4 Ω lamp in series with a 2 Ω lamp:
Example: total resistance and current • R1 = 4 Ω • R2 = 2 Ω • Rtotal = 4 Ω + 2 Ω = 6 Ω Battery voltage = 12 V Using I = V / R: I = 12 V / 6 Ω = 2 A
  • Ammeters usually have very small resistance, so we normally ignore their resistance in calculations.
6) Voltage across a single component
  • Once we know the current in the circuit, we can find the voltage across a single component using Ohm's law.
  • Remember: in a series circuit, the current through each component is the same.
Finding voltage across one component V = I × R • Use the circuit current and that component's resistance
  • Using the previous example with 2 A of current:
Example: voltage across each lamp • Lamp on the right: R = 2 Ω, I = 2 A V = I × R = 2 A × 2 Ω = 4 V • Total battery voltage = 12 V So the other lamp must have 12 V − 4 V = 8 V
  • Another way to find the voltage is to use a voltmeter.
  • A voltmeter is connected in parallel across the component we are interested in.
  • Even though the voltmeter is in parallel, we still treat the main circuit itself as a series circuit.
Important idea • Components with a greater resistance always get a greater share of the total voltage. • More resistance means more "push" is needed to move charge through that part of the circuit. • This is why the 4 Ω lamp had 8 V, while the 2 Ω lamp only had 4 V.
