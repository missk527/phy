Notes (Key points from the speech)

The motor effect
  • The motor effect is when a current-carrying wire experiences a force in a magnetic field.
  • A current-carrying wire produces its own magnetic field.
  • The wire’s magnetic field interacts with the external magnetic field.
Interaction of magnetic fields
  • The interaction between magnetic fields creates a force on the wire.
  • The wire is pushed out of the magnetic field.
  • The maximum force occurs when the wire is at 90° to the magnetic field.
Effect of wire angle
  • If the wire is at a smaller angle, the force becomes weaker.
  • If the wire is parallel to the magnetic field, there is no force.
Fleming’s left-hand rule
  • Fleming’s left-hand rule is used to find the direction of the force.
  • The first finger shows the direction of the magnetic field.
  • The second finger shows the direction of the current.
  • The thumb shows the direction of the force.
Magnetic field direction
  • The magnetic field direction goes from the north pole to the south pole.
  • The current direction must also be known to determine the force direction.
Horseshoe magnet example
  • A current-carrying rail between the poles of a horseshoe magnet experiences a force.
  • The side rails are fixed, but the connecting rail can move.
  • Using Fleming’s left-hand rule helps predict the movement direction.
Calculating force
  • When the wire is at 90° to the field, the force can be calculated using the equation:
  • F = BIL
  • F = force in Newtons, B = magnetic flux density in Teslas, I = current in amps, and L = wire length in meters.
Example calculation
  • A wire length of 10 cm must be converted to 0.1 m.
  • Using B = 0.4 T, I = 5 A, and L = 0.1 m:
  • F = 0.4 × 5 × 0.1 = 0.2 N.
