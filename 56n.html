Notes (Key points rewritten from the speech)

Newton’s First Law (inertia law)
  • A resultant force is required to change an object’s motion.
  • If the resultant force = 0: motion does not change.
  • Stationary object + zero resultant → stays stationary.
  • Moving object + zero resultant → continues at the same velocity (same speed & direction).
Newton’s Second Law
  • A non‑zero resultant force causes acceleration.
  • Depending on initial motion, acceleration can mean: start moving, speed up, slow down, stop, or change direction at constant speed (velocity changes because direction changes).
  • Proportionality: larger resultant force → larger acceleration (for the same mass).
  • Equation: F = m a. Rearranged: a = F / m.
Circular motion (constant speed, changing velocity)
  • Acceleration is “change in velocity per unit time”; velocity includes direction.
  • In uniform circular motion (e.g., the Moon’s orbit), speed is constant but direction constantly changes → object is accelerating.
  • Cause: a force perpendicular to motion (e.g., Earth’s gravity) provides the inward pull.
Worked example (from speech)
  • Mass m = 0.25 kg; forces: 30 N left, 42 N right.
  • Resultant: F = 42 − 30 = 12 N to the right.
  • Acceleration: a = F/m = 12 / 0.25 = 48 m·s⁻² (to the right).
Inertia & inertial mass
  • Inertia: tendency of an object’s motion to remain unchanged (rest stays at rest; motion stays in motion unless a resultant force acts).
  • Inertial mass: how hard it is to change velocity; defined via m = F / a (from Newton’s second law).
  • Large masses (e.g., the Moon) have large inertia → require big forces to change velocity; acceleration is small for a given force.
