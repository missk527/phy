38P. Exothermic & Endothermic Reactions

Key Notes (Synced with Video)

0:00 - 0:15 | Lesson Overview
  • This lesson explains exothermic and endothermic reactions.
  • Reaction profiles are used to show energy changes during reactions.
  • Activation energy is the energy needed to start a reaction.
0:15 - 0:51 | Energy Stored in Chemical Bonds
  • Different chemicals store different amounts of energy in their bonds.
  • The total energy of the reactants is compared with the total energy of the products.
  • For methane reacting with oxygen, the products have less energy than the reactants.
  • Energy changes depend on the difference between reactant energy and product energy.
0:51 - 1:57 | Exothermic Reactions
  • A reaction profile shows total energy on the y-axis and reaction progress on the x-axis.
  • Reactants are shown on the left and products on the right.
  • In an exothermic reaction, the products are lower because they have less energy.
  • The lost chemical energy is transferred to the surroundings, usually as heat.
  • Temperature increases when heat energy is released.
  • Exothermic reactions transfer energy to the surroundings.
2:06 - 2:26 | Examples of Exothermic Reactions
  • Combustion reactions are common exothermic reactions.
  • Fuels usually burn in oxygen during combustion.
  • Neutralisation between acids and bases is usually exothermic.
  • Most oxidation reactions are also exothermic.
2:26 - 3:15 | Endothermic Reactions
  • Endothermic reactions take in heat energy from the surroundings.
  • Calcium carbonate can be thermally decomposed into calcium oxide and carbon dioxide.
  • Heat must be supplied to break down calcium carbonate, for example using a Bunsen burner.
  • On an endothermic reaction profile, products are higher than reactants.
  • The vertical difference is labelled as energy absorbed.
3:15 - 4:31 | Activation Energy
  • Activation energy is the minimum energy reactant particles need to collide and react.
  • A higher activation energy means more energy is needed to start the reaction.
  • On a reaction profile, the curve rises from the reactants to a highest point.
  • The energy difference between the reactants and the top of the curve is the activation energy.
  • Even exothermic reactions need some energy to begin.
  • Endothermic reaction profiles also show activation energy in the same way.
4:31 - 4:54 | Changing and Labelling Profiles
  • Higher activation energy is shown by drawing a taller curve.
  • Lower activation energy is shown by drawing a lower curve.
  • For a specific reaction, write the actual chemicals instead of only writing “reactants” and “products”.
