Notes (Key points from the speech)

Particle theory (kinetic theory) model
  • Matter is made of tiny particles; we model them as small, solid, inelastic spheres.
  • The state (solid, liquid, gas) depends on particle arrangement, particle motion, and forces of attraction.
  • Heating generally increases particle energy (kinetic energy), while cooling reduces it.
Solids
  • Strong forces of attraction hold particles close together in fixed positions.
  • Particles form a regular lattice and can only vibrate about fixed points.
  • Solids have a definite shape and volume and do not flow.
Melting (solid → liquid)
  • Heating makes particles vibrate more and weakens attractions.
  • At the melting point, particles have enough energy to break free from fixed positions.
  • The substance changes into a liquid.
Liquids
  • Weaker attractions than solids, so particles can move past each other.
  • Particles are randomly arranged but still fairly close together (compact).
  • Liquids have a definite volume but no fixed shape, so they flow to fit a container.
Boiling / evaporation (liquid → gas)
  • Heating increases particle speed and further weakens attractions.
  • At the boiling point, particles have enough energy to fully overcome attractions.
  • The liquid turns into a gas (boils/evaporates).
Gases
  • Very weak attractions, so particles move freely and are far apart.
  • Gases have no definite shape or volume and always expand to fill their container.
  • Gas particles travel in straight lines, but their motion seems “random” because collisions change direction unpredictably.
Heating/cooling, pressure, mass, and density
  • Heating a gas makes particles move faster: the gas expands in a flexible container, or pressure increases in a rigid container.
  • Cooling a gas can cause condensation (gas → liquid); further cooling can cause freezing (liquid → solid).
  • In a closed system, state changes do not change mass (same number of particles).
  • Density usually: solid > liquid > gas (highest to lowest).
