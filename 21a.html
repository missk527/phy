Notes (Key points from the speech)

Two types of current
  • There are two key types of electric current you need to know: alternating current (AC) and direct current (DC).
  • The difference is the direction that charge flows in a circuit.
Alternating current (AC)
  • In AC, the direction of current constantly reverses (back and forth).
  • This happens when the potential difference is an alternating voltage that switches between positive and negative.
  • On a current–time or voltage–time graph, the values fluctuate above and below zero.
UK mains electricity
  • In the UK, the electricity from wall sockets (mains supply) is AC.
  • UK mains frequency is 50 Hz, meaning the voltage changes direction 50 times per second.
  • The mains voltage is around 240 V (you may also see 230 V; both are acceptable for exams).
  • So the voltage swings from about +240 V to −240 V and back repeatedly, and the current varies at the same rate.
Direct current (DC)
  • DC is produced by a direct potential difference that stays one sign (positive or negative) over time.
  • Charge flows in one direction only, so the current does not reverse.
  • DC is found in cells and batteries, such as those in phones and calculators.
Measuring voltage/current over time
  • To display potential difference or current against time, we use an oscilloscope.
  • An oscilloscope shows the waveform on a monitor, letting you see whether a signal is AC (alternating) or DC (constant).
