Visible & Ultraviolet Light: Uses

Video link: https://youtu.be/d_LgRTHiFQ8

Key Notes (rewritten from the speech)

1) Visible light basics
  • Visible light is the part of the electromagnetic spectrum that our eyes detect.
  • Different colours are different wavelengths: red has the longest wavelength and violet the shortest.
  • Use ROY G BIV to remember the order: red, orange, yellow, green, blue, indigo, violet.
2) Using visible light for communication
  • Visible (or near-visible) light can carry information through optical fibres (thin glass/plastic strands).
  • Data is sent as pulses of light that are encoded to represent information.
3) How optical fibres guide light
  • Light stays inside the fibre because it is reflected at the boundary again and again, so it “bounces” along the fibre to the other end.
  • To work well, the material must reflect the light rather than absorb it.
  • The reflections should be specular (mirror-like), not diffuse, so the light is not scattered.
4) Optical fibres vs copper wires
  • Compared with sending signals through copper wires, optical fibres can transmit more information.
  • Fibre signals are also less likely to be distorted during transmission.
5) Ultraviolet (UV) radiation
  • Ultraviolet radiation comes after violet on the spectrum and has a shorter wavelength than visible light.
  • Sunlight contains UV: it can cause suntan and sometimes sunburn.
  • We can also produce UV using lamps (e.g., sunbeds).
6) Fluorescence and fluorescent lamps
  • Fluorescence happens when a chemical absorbs UV and then re-emits the energy as visible light.
  • That’s why fluorescent paints look extremely bright: they are emitting light, not just reflecting it.
  • In a fluorescent bulb, UV is produced first; a phosphor coating inside the glass absorbs the UV and re-emits it as visible light.
7) Benefits and other uses of UV
  • Fluorescent bulbs are energy efficient, reducing electricity bills and carbon dioxide emissions.
  • UV is used in security marking: inks can be invisible in normal light but show up under UV (e.g., banknotes and passports).
  • UV can sterilise water by destroying microorganisms.
