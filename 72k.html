72. Absorbing and Emitting Radiation

Key Notes (English)

Adapted from the provided speech. Focus: how objects absorb and emit radiation, how temperature affects emission, emission graphs, and how these ideas apply to the Earth and its atmosphere.

1) Absorbing and Emitting Radiation
  • All objects continuously absorb and emit electromagnetic radiation to and from their surroundings.
  • When an object absorbs radiation, it gains energy and can warm up.
  • When it emits radiation, it loses energy and can cool down.
2) Radiation Balance and Temperature
  • An object's temperature depends on the balance between the radiation it absorbs and the radiation it emits.
  • If it emits more radiation than it absorbs, it cools down.
  • If it absorbs more radiation than it emits, it warms up.
  • If the absorbed and emitted amounts are equal, the temperature stays constant.
3) Examples: Hot and Cold Objects
  • A hot object such as a cup of tea emits more radiation than it absorbs, so it loses energy and cools down.
  • A cold object such as an ice cube absorbs more radiation than it emits, so it gains energy and warms up.
4) What Intensity Means
  • Intensity means the power of radiation transferred per unit area.
  • It tells us how much energy reaches a certain area in a given time.
5) Emission Graphs
  • Emission graphs show wavelength on the x-axis and intensity on the y-axis.
  • A higher intensity means more radiation is being emitted at that wavelength.
  • For this topic, the main regions are ultraviolet, visible light, and infrared.
6) Radiation from Room-Temperature Objects
  • Objects at room temperature, such as a paper bag, emit most of their radiation in the infrared region.
  • They do not emit much visible light, so we cannot see their emitted radiation.
7) Radiation from Hotter Objects
  • Hotter objects emit more radiation overall, so their curves are higher on the graph.
  • They also emit shorter wavelengths more strongly, so the curve shifts to the left.
  • A burning piece of coal emits more radiation than a cooler object.
8) Very Hot Objects
  • Extremely hot objects, such as the Sun, emit even more radiation and at even shorter wavelengths.
  • As temperature increases, the intensity increases across all wavelengths.
  • The increase is greater at shorter wavelengths.
9) Colour Changes with Temperature
  • As an object gets hotter, the wavelengths of light it emits become shorter.
  • This is why a Bunsen burner flame changes colour from orange-red to blue as it gets hotter.
10) Earth and Atmospheric Radiation
  • The Earth absorbs electromagnetic radiation from the Sun and also emits infrared radiation.
  • The atmosphere can reflect, absorb, and emit radiation too.
  • During the day, more energy is absorbed than emitted locally, so temperature rises.
  • At night, less energy is absorbed than emitted locally, so temperature falls.
  • Overall, the Earth's average temperature stays fairly constant because some part of the planet is always receiving sunlight.
