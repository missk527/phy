29k. Specific Latent Heat

Key Notes (English)

Adapted from the provided speech. Focus: specific latent heat, state changes, heating/cooling graphs, and energy calculations.

1) Internal Energy and Temperature
  • When a substance is heated, its particles gain energy in their kinetic energy store.
  • This increases the substance’s internal energy.
  • Temperature is a measure of the average internal energy of the particles.
  • So, when internal energy increases during normal heating, the temperature rises.
2) The Exception During a Change of State
  • During a change of state, temperature does not increase even though energy is still being supplied.
  • At the melting point or boiling point, the energy is used to weaken or break the forces between particles.
  • Because that energy is not increasing the average internal energy in the usual way, the temperature stays constant until the state change is complete.
3) Cooling Graphs and Constant Temperature
  • The same idea applies during cooling.
  • When a substance changes state while cooling, new bonds form between particles.
  • This process releases energy, which counteracts the cooling for a time.
  • As a result, the temperature remains constant during the state change.
4) Meaning of Latent Heat
  • Latent heat is the energy required to change the state of a substance without changing its temperature.
  • The amount of latent heat depends on:
  • 1. the type of substance, and
  • 2. the amount (mass) of the substance.
5) Specific Latent Heat (SLH)
  • Specific latent heat standardises the idea by referring to 1 kilogram of a substance.
  • It is the energy required to change the state of 1 kg of a substance without changing its temperature.
  • For cooling, it can also be described as the energy released when 1 kg changes state.
6) Two Types of Specific Latent Heat
  • Specific latent heat of fusion is the energy change when a substance changes between a solid and a liquid.
  • It applies to melting and freezing.
  • Specific latent heat of vaporization is the energy change when a substance changes between a liquid and a gas.
  • It applies to boiling / evaporation and condensation.
7) Water Example from -50°C to 150°C
  • Start with 1 kg of ice at -50°C: heating increases internal energy, so the temperature rises up to 0°C.
  • At 0°C, the ice melts and the temperature stays constant while energy is used to break forces between particles.
  • For water, the specific latent heat of fusion is 334,000 J/kg.
  • After all the ice has melted, the liquid water warms from 0°C to 100°C.
  • At 100°C, the water boils and the temperature again stays constant until all of it becomes water vapour.
  • For water, the specific latent heat of vaporization is 2,260,000 J/kg.
  • After boiling is complete, the steam temperature rises above 100°C.
8) Heating and Cooling Use the Same Values
  • For heating, latent heat values describe the energy required for a state change.
  • For cooling, the same values describe the energy released during the reverse change of state.
  • So the amount is the same, but the energy transfer direction is different.
9) Specific Latent Heat Equation
  • The equation is E = mL.
  • E = energy transferred in joules (J)
  • m = mass in kilograms (kg)
  • L = specific latent heat in joules per kilogram (J/kg)
  • This equation can be used for energy required or energy released.
10) Example Calculation
  • Question: How much energy is needed to completely boil 2.5 kg of water at 100°C?
  • Because the water is boiling, use the specific latent heat of vaporization.
  • Use E = mL → E = 2.5 × 2,260,000.
  • The answer is 5,650,000 J.
  • This can also be written as 5,650 kJ.
  • In exams, the exact latent heat values would be given if needed.
