Velocity–Time Graphs — Gradient = Acceleration, Area = Distance

Notes (rewritten in English from the speech)

V–t vs. d–t graphs
  • Velocity–time (v–t) graphs show how velocity changes with time (y‑axis: velocity; x‑axis: time).
  • Distance–time (d–t) graphs show how distance changes with time.
  • They look similar; in exams, double‑check which graph you’re reading.
v on y‑axis t on x‑axis Don’t confuse with d–t
Gradient → acceleration
  • Gradient on a v–t graph is Δv / Δt → that is the acceleration.
  • Positive constant gradient → constant acceleration.
  • Negative constant gradient → constant deceleration.
  • Zero gradient (flat) → constant velocity (no acceleration).
  • Steeper curve → increasing gradient → increasing acceleration.
  • Example: Δv = 3 m/s over Δt = 2 s ⇒ a = 1.5 m/s².
Reading values
  • For a flat section, the velocity equals the y‑axis value.
  • Example: stage 2 velocity 3 m/s; stage 4 velocity 5 m/s.
Area under the curve → distance
  • The area under a v–t graph gives the distance travelled.
  • Break the area into simple shapes (triangles/rectangles) to calculate.
  • Example (first 4 s): triangle area = ½ × base(2 s) × height(3 m/s) = 3 m; rectangle area = base(2 s) × height(3 m/s) = 6 m; total 9 m.
  • Units: although it’s an area, we report the distance in metres (m), not m².
Estimating curved areas
  • If the graph has curved sections and a grid is provided, estimate by counting squares under the curve.
  • Combine partial squares to make full ones.
  • Example: ~6 full squares + two halves + one nearly full ≈ ~8 m over 2 seconds (given 1 square = 1 m).
