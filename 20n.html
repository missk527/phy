The National Grid

Key Notes (rewritten from the speech)

What the National Grid is
  • The National Grid is a large network of cables, pylons, and transformers that distributes electricity across the country.
  • It allows electricity generated in power stations to be transmitted efficiently to homes and businesses.
Where electricity comes from
  • In the UK, electricity is generated from several sources, but a large proportion is produced in power stations.
  • Power stations (whether using fossil fuels or nuclear fission) typically generate heat first, then convert thermal energy into electrical energy.
Demand changes throughout the day
  • Electricity generation depends on demand.
  • Demand often peaks in the late afternoon and evening when people return home and use appliances (cooking, TVs, etc.).
  • To handle demand surges, power stations keep spare capacity and may run below their maximum output.
Power, voltage, and current
  • Electrical power depends on voltage and current (power = voltage × current).
  • To transmit huge amounts of power across long distances, the grid must manage voltage and current carefully.
Why high current causes energy loss
  • If a large current flows through transmission wires, the wires heat up due to their resistance.
  • This heating wastes energy to the surroundings, making long-distance transmission inefficient.
Why the grid uses very high voltage
  • To reduce energy loss, the grid keeps the current low.
  • For the same power, lowering current means the voltage must be increased.
  • So electricity leaving a power station is sent at a very high voltage.
Step-up transformers
  • Step-up transformers increase the voltage before transmission.
  • The voltage is stepped up to about 400,000 V for efficient long-distance transfer.
Transmission and pylons
  • High-voltage electricity is carried over long distances along overhead lines on large pylons.
  • This connects regions across the country so electricity can be delivered where it is needed.
Step-down transformers and safety
  • Near towns and homes, step-down transformers reduce the voltage.
  • The voltage is lowered to around 230 V for domestic use.
  • Lowering voltage is essential because high voltages are dangerous and would damage household appliances.
Overall summary
  • Electricity is generated in power stations, stepped up to high voltage, transmitted across the country, then stepped down to a safe voltage for homes.
  • The National Grid’s main purpose is to reduce energy loss during transmission while delivering electricity safely.
