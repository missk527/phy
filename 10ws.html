Wind & Solar Power — How they work, pros & cons

Notes (rewritten in English from the speech)

How wind power works
  • Wind energy is captured by wind turbines, often seen in exposed areas like moors or coasts.
  • Spinning blades turn a generator, converting kinetic energy from wind into electrical energy.
  • Electricity is sent to the national grid.
Exposed sites Blades → generator Kinetic → electrical
How solar power works
  • Solar cells (solar panels) generate electric current directly from sunlight.
  • Good for low‑energy devices (watches, calculators) to avoid battery replacement.
  • Useful in remote regions not connected to the main grid (e.g., the Australian outback).
  • Now widely used at large scale to power entire towns.
Pros shared by wind & solar
  • Very low running costs once installed.
  • No pollutants during operation — notably no CO₂.
Cons & limitations
  • High upfront costs; manufacturing (especially of solar cells) can produce pollutants.
  • Weather‑dependent: no wind or no sun → no electricity.
  • No on‑demand ramping: cannot instantly increase supply at peak demand.
  • Require large areas; need many units to generate significant power.
  • Public concerns: visual impact and noise (for wind turbines).
Bottom line
  • Neither wind nor solar damages the environment during use.
  • Both are becoming cheaper every year.
