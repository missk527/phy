Forces, Momentum & Safety Features

Key Notes (rewritten from the speech)

1) Momentum recap
  • Momentum is defined as mass × velocity.
Momentum p = m × v p = momentum (kg m/s) m = mass (kg) v = velocity (m/s)
  • Example: a 2 kg object travelling at 5 m/s has momentum:
    • p = 2 × 5 = 10 kg m/s
  • If we apply a force in the same direction as motion, the object accelerates and its velocity increases.
  • Higher velocity means the object’s momentum increases.
  • Example: if the same 2 kg object speeds up to 8 m/s:
    • p = 2 × 8 = 16 kg m/s
2) Force and change in momentum
  • A force can change an object’s momentum by speeding it up or slowing it down.
  • The size of the force depends on:
    How big the change in momentum is (Δp) How long the change takes (Δt)
Force and change in momentum F = Δp / Δt F = force (N) Δp = change in momentum (kg m/s) Δt = time taken for the change (s)
  • Example: the 2 kg object changes momentum from 10 to 16 kg m/s in 0.5 s:
    • Initial momentum pinitial = 10 kg m/s
    • Final momentum pfinal = 16 kg m/s
    • Δp = 16 − 10 = 6 kg m/s
    • Δt = 0.5 s
    • F = Δp / Δt = 6 / 0.5 = 12 N
  • So the hand applied a force of 12 N.
Alternative form F = (mv − mu) / t m = mass u = initial velocity v = final velocity mv = final momentum mu = initial momentum
3) Worked example: accelerating a ball
  • Question: Calculate the force needed to accelerate a 600 g ball from rest to 14 m/s in 0.1 s.
  • Step 1 – Write known values:
    • Initial velocity u = 0 m/s (it starts at rest)
    • Final velocity v = 14 m/s
    • Mass m = 600 g = 0.6 kg
    • Time t = 0.1 s
Using F = (mv − mu) / t • m = 0.6 kg • v = 14 m/s • u = 0 m/s • t = 0.1 s Final momentum mv = 0.6 × 14 = 8.4 kg m/s Initial momentum mu = 0.6 × 0 = 0 kg m/s Δp = 8.4 − 0 = 8.4 kg m/s F = Δp / t = 8.4 / 0.1 = 84 N
  • Answer: the required force is 84 N.
4) Car crashes and large forces
  • This idea is especially important in car crashes.
  • If a car travelling at speed hits a wall and stops almost instantly:
    • its momentum decreases to zero in a very short time,
    • so Δt is very small.
Crash example • Large change in momentum (from high value to zero). • Very short time for the change. → F = Δp / Δt becomes very large.
  • A very large force acts on the people in the car.
  • This can cause serious injuries such as head trauma or spinal damage.
5) Safety features: increasing impact time
  • To reduce injuries, we want to make the momentum change happen over a longer time.
Key safety idea • If Δp is the same but Δt is bigger, F = Δp / Δt will be smaller. • A longer impact time → smaller force → less chance of injury.
  • Modern cars are designed with safety features that increase the time taken to stop.
  • Examples:
    Crumple zones Seat belts Airbags
Crumple zones • Located at the front and back of the car. • Crumple on impact instead of stopping instantly. • Provide extra time for the car and passengers to lose momentum.
Seat belts • Stop you from being thrown out of the car. • Slightly stretchy so you slow down more gradually. • Increase the time taken for your momentum to fall to zero.
Airbags • Inflate in front of you during a crash. • Soft, compressible cushion instead of a hard dashboard or steering wheel. • Increase the time of impact and spread the force over a larger area.
6) Other safety systems and the key principle
  • Other safety systems work in a similar way, for example:
    Helmets Crash mats
Examples • Helmets: padding compresses during impact, increasing impact time. • Crash mats: soft, thick surfaces that slow you down more gently when you land.
  • The key principle is the same in every case:
Core idea to remember • All these safety features increase the time taken for momentum to change. • A longer time means a smaller force (for the same change in momentum). • Smaller forces reduce the risk of harm.
