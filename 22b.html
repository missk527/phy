Notes (Key points rewritten from the speech)

What are nanoparticles?
  • Nanoparticles are extremely tiny particles with a diameter of about 1–100 nanometres (nm).
  • They are far too small to be seen with a light microscope.
  • The science that studies them is called nanoscience.
Why do nanoparticles behave differently?
  • A key feature is their very large surface area to volume ratio.
  • As objects get smaller, volume decreases faster than surface area, so the ratio increases.
  • Many applications depend on exposed surface area, not on bulk volume.
Use 1: Catalysts
  • Nanoparticles can act as catalysts to speed up chemical reactions.
  • Only the surface is involved, so nanoparticles can be effective using much less material than larger particles.
Use 2: Nanomedicine
  • Nanomedicine uses nanoparticles in medical treatments.
  • Scientists are researching fullerenes (spheres made of carbon atoms) to carry drugs around the body.
  • Because they are so small, the aim is to deliver drugs directly into cells.
Use 3: Electronics
  • Some nanoparticles can conduct electricity.
  • This could allow very tiny electrical circuits and potentially smaller computer chips.
Use 4: Antibacterial materials
  • Silver nanoparticles have antibacterial properties.
  • They can be used in items like surgical masks and wound dressings to help kill bacteria and reduce infection risk.
Risks and uncertainties
  • Nanotechnology is still relatively new, so long‑term effects on the body are not fully understood.
  • There is no clear evidence that nanoparticles cause harm, but more testing is needed before we can be confident.
  • Some people argue for stricter regulation and clearer labelling of products that may contain nanoparticles.
Example: Nanoparticles in sunscreen
  • Nanoparticles in sunscreen can improve protection because they can give better skin coverage against UV rays.
  • However, it is uncertain whether nanoparticles can enter the body and, if they do, whether they could damage cells.
  • It is also unclear what happens when they are washed into the sea and whether they could harm the environment.
Take‑home message
  • Nanoparticles offer many useful applications because of their unique size‑dependent properties.
  • At the same time, we need more evidence about health and environmental impacts to use them safely.
