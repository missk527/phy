Notes (Key points from the speech)

What the electromagnetic spectrum is
  • The electromagnetic (EM) spectrum is the full range of electromagnetic waves.
  • All EM waves are part of one continuous spectrum; we split them into categories for convenience.
Wave type and speed
  • All EM waves are transverse: they oscillate perpendicular to the direction of energy transfer.
  • In a vacuum, all EM waves travel at the same speed: 3 × 10⁸ m/s.
  • In different media, EM waves travel at different speeds, which can cause refraction (a change in direction).
Wavelength and frequency
  • EM waves are distinguished by their wavelength and frequency.
  • Wavelength and frequency are inversely related: as frequency increases, wavelength decreases.
  • Across the spectrum: radio waves have the longest wavelength and lowest frequency; gamma rays have the shortest wavelength and highest frequency.
  • Typical wavelengths range from kilometres (radio) to less than 10⁻³ nanometres (gamma).
Visible light and colour
  • Human eyes detect only a tiny part of the spectrum: visible light.
  • Different wavelengths in the visible region produce different colours.
  • Colour order can be remembered as: red, orange, yellow, green, blue, indigo, violet (ROYGBIV).
Order of the EM spectrum
  • A helpful way to memorise the spectrum is to start at visible light and work outward.
  • Beyond violet is ultraviolet; beyond red is infrared.
  • At the high‑frequency end are ultraviolet, X‑rays, and gamma rays, which can be ionising and damage cells.
  • At the low‑frequency end are microwaves and radio waves, widely used for communication.
Where EM waves come from
  • EM waves are produced by many processes “from everywhere”. Examples mentioned:
  • Gamma rays can be emitted during radioactive decay.
  • Visible light, ultraviolet, and X‑rays can be emitted when electrons drop to lower energy levels.
  • Infrared can be produced when molecular bonds vibrate.
What happens when waves meet matter
  • After emission, EM waves can travel through empty space.
  • When they hit a material, they may be reflected, absorbed, or transmitted—often as a combination.
