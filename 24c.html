Static Electricity

Video link: https://youtu.be/wzvIeRLy5Nw

Key Notes (rewritten from the speech)

1) What static electricity is
  • Static electricity is a build-up of electric charge on an insulator.
  • When the charge is released (discharged), it can cause a spark.
2) Why most objects are neutral
  • All materials contain positive protons and negative electrons.
  • In a neutral object, total positive charge equals total negative charge, so the overall charge is zero.
3) Charging by friction (rubbing)
  • Rubbing two materials creates friction that can transfer electrons from one material to the other.
  • Only electrons move: protons stay fixed in the nucleus.
  • If an object gains electrons, it becomes negatively charged. If it loses electrons, it becomes positively charged.
4) Conductors vs insulators
  • In conductors (e.g., metals), electrons can move freely, so charge usually flows away and doesn’t build up easily.
  • In insulators (e.g., plastic rods, cloth), electrons cannot move freely, so charge can stay on the surface and build up.
5) Examples of electron transfer
  • Polythene rod + cloth: electrons move onto the polythene → polythene becomes negative, cloth becomes positive.
  • Acetate rod + cloth: electrons move off the acetate → acetate becomes positive, cloth becomes negative.
  • Which way electrons transfer depends on the materials involved.
6) Sparks and earthing
  • As charge builds up, a potential difference can form between the charged object and the Earth (or an earthed object).
  • Earth is treated as 0 V.
  • If the potential difference is big enough, electrons can jump the gap to discharge — this is a spark.
7) Charge build-up on conductors (car example)
  • Charge can sometimes build up on conductors (less common), e.g., a car’s metal frame can gain charge through contact with moving air.
  • Touching the car can let it discharge through you to Earth, causing a small electric shock (a tiny spark).
