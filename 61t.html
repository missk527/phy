Waves: Parts, Speed, Frequency, Transverse vs Longitudinal

Notes (rewritten in English from the speech)

What waves do
  • Waves transfer energy from one place to another, but they do not transfer matter.
  • Examples: light from a phone screen to your eye, and sound from a speaker to your ear.
  • That transferred energy can carry information (images, music, speech).

Wave graphs and key terms
  • Waves oscillate about an equilibrium position.
  • On a displacement–distance graph:
    • Distance (x-axis) = how far the wave has travelled from the start.
    • Displacement (y-axis) = how far the wave is from equilibrium.
  • Amplitude = the maximum displacement from equilibrium.
  • Wavelength (λ) = the distance of one complete oscillation (e.g., crest to crest).
  • Crest = highest point; trough = lowest point.
Time period and frequency
  • On a displacement–time graph, the length of one full oscillation is the time period (T), not the wavelength.
  • Time period (T) = time for one complete oscillation.
  • Frequency (f) = number of oscillations per second (unit: hertz, Hz).
  • Key equations: f = 1 / T and T = 1 / f.
  • Example: if T = 0.5 s, then f = 2 Hz. If f = 4 Hz, then T = 0.25 s.
Wave speed
  • Wave speed is given by: v = f × λ.
  • Interpretation: wavelength (meters per wave) × how many waves per second = meters per second.
  • Example: f = 400 Hz, λ = 70 cm = 0.70 m → v = 400 × 0.70 = 280 m/s.
Transverse vs longitudinal waves
  • Transverse waves: oscillations are perpendicular to the direction of energy transfer.
  • Examples of transverse waves: electromagnetic waves (light, radio), water ripples, waves on strings.
  • Longitudinal waves: oscillations are parallel to the direction of energy transfer.
  • They form regions of compressions and rarefactions as particles vibrate back and forth.
  • Examples of longitudinal waves: sound waves and seismic P-waves.
