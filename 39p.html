39P. Nuclear Fusion

Key Notes (Synced with Video)

0:00 - 0:27 | What Nuclear Fusion Is
  • Nuclear fusion happens when two light nuclei join together to form one larger nucleus.
  • For example, hydrogen-1 and hydrogen-2 nuclei can fuse to form helium-3.
  • Fusion releases a very large amount of energy.
  • This is the process that fuels stars and helped create elements heavier than hydrogen.
0:32 - 1:23 | Why Fusion Releases So Much Energy
  • During fusion, the new nucleus has slightly less mass than the original nuclei combined.
  • The missing mass is converted into energy rather than staying in the product nucleus.
  • Einstein’s equation E = mc² explains how mass can be changed into energy.
  • Because the speed of light squared is extremely large, even a tiny mass loss releases a huge amount of energy.
1:27 - 1:58 | Benefits and Challenges of Fusion
  • Nuclear fusion does not produce radioactive waste in the same way nuclear fission does.
  • Hydrogen fuel is relatively easy to obtain.
  • Fusion needs extremely high temperatures and pressures, around 10 million degrees.
  • Scientists are still researching how to make fusion practical on Earth.
1:58 - 2:38 | Fusion Compared with Fission
  • Nuclear fusion joins light nuclei to make heavier nuclei and releases a huge amount of energy.
  • Nuclear fission splits large unstable nuclei into smaller nuclei.
  • Fission also releases energy, but less than fusion.
  • Nuclear fission is the process currently used on Earth to generate electricity in nuclear power stations.
