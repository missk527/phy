Notes (Key points rewritten from the speech)

1) How our energy use has changed
  • For most of human history, the primary energy source was wood (heating, cooking).
  • Over the last ~200 years, heavy use of non‑renewables: fossil fuels (coal, oil, natural gas) and nuclear energy.
  • In the past ~50 years, rapid growth of renewables: solar, wind, hydroelectric, geothermal, biofuels, tidal, etc.
2) What we use energy for (UK focus)
  • Two major sectors: transport and domestic.
  • Transport = cars, trains, planes, ships. Traditionally powered by oil‑derived fuels (petrol, diesel; kerosene for planes).
  • Trains: historically coal (industrial revolution), now mostly electric.
  • Recent trends: more biofuels and growth of electric cars.
  • Domestic = household heating & cooking.
3) Heating at home
  • Main UK heating source: natural gas burned to heat water, then pumped through radiators to warm rooms (thermal energy transfer to air).
  • Other non‑renewable heating: coal burned in fireplaces.
  • Renewable heating options: solar water heaters (sun heats water → radiators), and biofuels burned similarly to coal.
4) Electricity: not renewable or non‑renewable by itself
  • Electricity is a carrier, not a resource. Its status depends on how it is generated.
  • Example breakdown (speech, UK): ~60% from fossil fuels; non‑renewables in total ≈ ~80% → most UK electricity from non‑renewable sources.
5) Key definitions
  • Non‑renewable: resources that will run out (fossil fuels, nuclear fuel).
  • Renewable: resources replenished on human timescales (e.g., solar, wind, hydro, geothermal, biofuels, tidal).
  • Biofuels: fuels made from biomass (plants/organic matter); considered renewable.
