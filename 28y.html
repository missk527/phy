Carbon Allotropes: Graphene & Fullerenes

Key Notes (rewritten from the speech)

1) Allotropes of carbon
  • Allotropes are different structural forms of the same element in the same physical state.
  • Here, we focus on solid carbon and how changing the arrangement of carbon atoms changes the material’s properties.
  • Graphite, graphene, and fullerenes are all carbon allotropes, just built in different ways.
2) Graphene: a single layer from graphite
  • Graphite is made of many stacked layers.
  • Each single layer is called graphene.
  • Graphene is a flat sheet of carbon atoms arranged in repeating hexagons.
  • Each carbon atom bonds to three other carbon atoms, creating a very strong network.
3) Why graphene can conduct electricity
  • In graphene, each carbon atom contributes one electron to a delocalised (shared) pool of electrons.
  • These electrons can move through the sheet, so graphene can conduct electricity.
  • This makes graphene useful for electronics and other advanced technologies.
  • Graphene is natural because we can obtain it from graphite (found in the ground).
Graphene — key properties • One-atom-thick carbon sheet • Hexagonal lattice • Very strong • Conducts electricity (delocalised electrons)
4) Fullerenes: carbon cages, spheres, and tubes
  • Scientists can shape carbon sheets into hollow structures such as spheres and tubes.
  • Including pentagons or heptagons in the pattern can make the sheet curve.
  • These hollow carbon shapes are called fullerenes.
  • A famous example is Buckminsterfullerene, a hollow sphere made of 60 carbon atoms (formula C60).
  • Very tiny tubes are called carbon nanotubes.
5) Uses of fullerenes and nanotubes (nanotechnology)
  • Drug delivery: fullerene spheres can form a “cage” around drug molecules to help deliver them to specific places in the body.
  • Catalysts: fullerenes have a very high surface area to volume ratio, making them useful as industrial catalysts.
  • Electronics & nanotechnology: nanotubes can conduct electricity and are used in nano-scale devices.
  • Strengthening materials: nanotubes are long and thin (high length-to-diameter ratio), so they can add strength without adding much weight (e.g., sports equipment).
  • Nanotechnology is the use of extremely small structures like these across many industries (medicine, batteries, food, fashion, and more).
Main takeaway • Graphene: a single, strong, conductive carbon layer. • Fullerenes: hollow carbon shapes (spheres/tubes) with many practical uses.
