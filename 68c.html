Key Notes (rewritten from the speech)

1) Where X-rays and gamma rays sit in the EM spectrum

X-rays and gamma rays are electromagnetic waves at the extreme high-energy end of the spectrum, with very short wavelengths and very high frequencies. Because they are high energy, they are also ionising radiation.

2) How an X-ray image is made

In a hospital X-ray, radiation is fired through the body and a detector plate records how much gets through. Different tissues absorb different amounts, so the detector receives different intensities and forms an image of internal structures.

3) Why bones look white and lungs look dark

Dense materials like bone absorb X-rays strongly, so fewer reach the detector and those regions stay white. Air-filled areas (lungs/intestines) let X-rays pass through more easily, so the detector darkens and those regions appear darker. Soft tissues often appear grey because they absorb partially.

4) Uses and safety of X-rays

X-rays are used to spot broken bones and help diagnose diseases. The patient dose is usually low, so the benefit often outweighs the risk, but staff reduce exposure by using lead shielding and standing back or leaving the room during exposure.

5) Gamma rays for sterilisation

Gamma rays can kill microorganisms, so they are used to sterilise medical equipment and food. Unlike boiling, gamma sterilisation does not rely on high temperature, so it can sterilise materials (like some plastics) without heat damage.

6) The medical trade-off

Because X-rays and gamma rays are ionising, they can damage cells and in rare cases contribute to cancer. In medicine we weigh the risk against the diagnostic or treatment benefit for each individual situation.
