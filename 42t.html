84B. Free Body Diagrams and Resultant Force

Key Notes (Synced with Video)

0:00 - 0:10 | Introduction to Free Body Diagrams
  • This video explains free body diagrams and how they help us find the resultant force on an object.
  • A free body diagram is a simple drawing that shows every force acting on one object.
  • Forces are shown using arrows.
0:23 - 0:52 | Forces on a Flying Plane
  • A plane in flight can be represented with four main force arrows.
  • Thrust acts forwards, while air resistance or drag acts backwards.
  • Weight acts downwards, and lift acts upwards.
0:52 - 1:14 | Magnitude and Direction
  • Forces are vectors, so each force has both magnitude and direction.
  • The direction is shown by the way the arrow points.
  • The magnitude is shown by the arrow length and can be labelled in newtons.
1:14 - 1:35 | Resultant Force
  • Forces acting in opposite directions may cancel each other out.
  • After balanced forces are removed, the remaining overall force is called the resultant force.
  • The resultant force describes the overall force acting on the object.
1:35 - 2:26 | Calculating Resultant Force
  • It is easiest to calculate horizontal and vertical forces separately.
  • In the vertical direction, 80,000 N up and 80,000 N down give a resultant force of 0 N.
  • In the horizontal direction, 120,000 N right and 90,000 N left give a resultant force of 30,000 N to the right.
  • Overall, the plane has a resultant force of 30,000 N to the right.
2:39 - 3:01 | Balanced Forces and Equilibrium
  • If air resistance is 120,000 N, it balances the 120,000 N thrust.
  • The horizontal resultant force is then 0 N.
  • When both horizontal and vertical forces are balanced, the object is in equilibrium.
  • An object in equilibrium has no resultant force acting on it.
